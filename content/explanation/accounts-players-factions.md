---
title: Accounts, players, and factions
weight: 1
---

Epimethean Challenge (EC) keeps three ideas apart that games often blur together:
*who you are*, *your place in a particular game*, and *the thing you command
there*. They are the **account**, the **player**, and the **faction**. One
sentence holds them together: *your account lets you log in; in each game you join
you take a player's seat and found a faction to command.*

It is tempting to collapse all three into a single word — most people would just
call the whole bundle "the player." EC pulls them apart on purpose, because each
answers a different question and each has a different lifetime.

Your [**account**]({{< relref "/reference/account.md" >}}) is *you*, across
everything. It carries the email and password you log in with, and it is the same
whether you play one game or a dozen at once. Nothing about a single game lives
here; the account is your standing identity in EC.

A [**player**]({{< relref "/reference/players.md" >}}) is a *seat in one game*.
When your account joins a game, it takes a player seat there — and everything that
only makes sense inside that game belongs to the seat, not to you-in-general: a
per-game id, whether you are still active, and the faction you command. This is
why EC can say a player id is assigned in order and never reused. That rule was
always about the seat, never about the person; a sentence that reads oddly when
you imagine it applying to a human reads plainly once you see it is about a chair
at a particular table. You can hold seats in several games at once, each with its
own id, all reached through the one account.

A [**faction**]({{< relref "/reference/faction.md" >}}) is *what you command in
the game* — the power that holds systems, has orders issued for it, and receives a
turn report. It is the piece other players see and contend with. Where the player
is your seat, the faction is what that seat reaches out and does. A faction, too,
belongs to exactly one game; it is meaningless outside it.

A faction is not handed to you the moment you sit down. After joining, you *found*
your faction as a setup step — until then your seat commands nothing, and the
GM's seat never commands anything at all. Once founded, a faction is durable: it
can be ground down to nothing and still exist, and if you leave the game it does
not vanish with you but carries on **independent**, with no commanding player, for
others to encounter. The bond between a player and a faction is real but not
eternal — it begins at founding and ends when the player departs.

Separating the three is what dissolves an old confusion. The account holds the
global identity; the player holds the per-game identity and state; the faction
holds the game-world power. Each thing EC says about "the player" turns out to be
true — it was just attached to the wrong noun.

## One player runs the game

Not every player commands a faction. One seat in each game belongs to the **game
master (GM)** — a player whose GM flag is set. The GM manages the game:
generating the cluster, processing turns, and looking after the other players. The
GM commands no faction of her own. She is still a player — a seat in one game,
reached through an account — just one whose job is to run the table rather than to
sit at it as a rival.
